Fourier Series

December 26, 2025 · Donggyu Lee

The Motivating Problem#

In this post, I pose a motivating problem for undergrad level real analysis. In the 19th century, French mathematician Joseph Fourier striked the math community with the claim that:

Fourier’s Claim

Every reasonable periodic function $f$ can be expressed with (infinite) linear combination of sines and cosines. That is, $$f(x) \sim a_0 + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} (a_n\cos nx + b_n\sin nx)$$

Here, reasonable allows sharp corners and discontinuities. But intuitively, can an infinite sum of smooth functions result in such cases? How far can we get with sine and cosine? Follows the main question of this post: suppose sine and cosine are the basis of a function space $X$. What exactly is $X$, and how do we prove this?

Notation#

Before anything, note that using exponential form tidies up the notation. Using Euler’s formula, the conversion

$$a_0 + \sum_{n=1}^{N} (a_n\cos nx + b_n\sin nx) = \sum_{n=-N}^{N}c_ne^{inx}$$

can be made, where $a_n, b_n, c_n \in \mathbb{C}$. Finding the expression of ${ c_n: n\in\mathbb{Z} }$ in terms of $a_n, b_n$ is left as an exercise.

The $\mathcal{R}^2$ Space#

Our goal is to find what type of function space will $\lbrace e^{inx}: n\in \mathbb{Z} \rbrace$ be a basis of. In order to make sense of approximating various functions, our function space $X$ must be an (complex) inner product space. As an extension of finite space inner products, it is natural to define it as:

$$\langle f, g\rangle = \int_{I}f\bar{g}$$

Since we deal with periodic functions, we can put $I=[-\pi, \pi]$ $The norm follows as:

$$||f|| = \int_I |f|^2 $$

For this norm to be well-defined, it seems natural to limit $X$ to functions such that $ \int_I |f|^2 < \infty$. We re-name this functioal space $X$ as the $\mathcal{R}^2$ space, or more specifically, $\mathcal{R}^2[-\pi, \pi]$.

Orthogonality#

The subset $\lbrace e^{inx}: n\in \mathbb{Z} \rbrace \subset \mathcal{R}^2[-\pi, \pi] $ is orthogonal, which is easy to see via direct algebra. For simplicity, let us define $u_n = e^{inx}$. Then, with $n\neq m,$

$$ \begin{aligned} \langle u_n, u_m \rangle &= \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} e^{inx} e^{-imx} dx \\ &= \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} e^{i(n-m)x} dx \\ &= 0 \quad \because\text{\small full cycle} \end{aligned} $$ whereas $n=m$ leads to $\langle u_n, u_m \rangle = 2\pi$. Thus, $\lbrace e^{inx}: n\in \mathbb{Z} \rbrace$ becomes an orthonormal subset if we redefine the inner product as

$$\langle f, g\rangle = \cfrac{1}{2\pi}\int_{I}f\bar{g}.$$

Bessel’s Inequality#

Given any $f\in \mathcal{R}^2[-\pi, \pi]$, how should we express, or approximate, it with our orthonormal subset $\lbrace e^{inx}: n\in \mathbb{Z} \rbrace$? Formally, which coefficients $\lbrace c_n \rbrace$ will minimize the distance between $f$ and $\sum c_n u_n$? With insights from linear algebra, the answer is the projection of $f$ on $\text{span}\lbrace e^{inx}\rbrace$. That is,

Proposition 1.

$c_n = \langle f, u_n\rangle$ leads to the minimum distance. For other arbitrary coefficients $\lbrace z_n \rbrace$, $$\left\lVert f - \sum_{n=-N}^{N} c_n u_n \right\rVert \leq \left\lVert f - \sum_{n=-N}^{N} z_n u_n \right\rVert, \quad {\small N=1,2,\dots} $$
